Jazz for Kookz

Happy birthday, Kyle! You said you wanted an intro to jazz, so here it is.

  • Kind of Blue

    My favorite jazz album is Kind of Blue (1959) by trumpet legend Miles Davis.

    One of the most famous jazz albums in history, with a number of tunes having since become "standards" (tunes frequently covered by other jazz artists). On the recording are jazz virtuosos Bill Evans (piano), John Coltrane (tenor sax), Julian "Cannonball" Adderley (alto sax), Paul Chambers (bass), and Jimmy Cobb (drums).

  • Moanin'

    Another great album is Moanin' (1958) by Art Blakey and the Jazz Messengers.

    The Jazz Messengers started out as a collective in the early 50s and ended when their founder (Art Blakey) died in 1990. Art was a phenomenal drummer that played with the likes of Thelonious Monk and Charlie Parker. The Messengers transformed into somewhat of a school for up and coming jazz artists to hone their craft and prove their worth.

    Many Messenger alumni went on to become jazz stars in their own right, such as: Lee Morgan, Benny Golson, Wayne Shorter, Freddie Hubbard, Bobby Timmons, Curtis Fuller, Cedar Walton, Chuck Mangione, Keith Jarrett, Joanne Brackeen, Woody Shaw, Wynton Marsalis, Branford Marsalis, Terence Blanchard, Donald Harrison, Mulgrew Miller, and Benny Green.

  • Solo Monk

    One of my favorite jazz pianists is Thelonious Monk. You have probably heard me play some of his tunes. Most newcomers to jazz have a hard time enjoying Monk, because of his signature usage of the whole tone scale and how he often played "out" of the scale the song was "in".

    That being said, he has some beautiful works that even new jazz listeners will love. One I recommend is "Ruby, My Dear" from his album Solo Monk.

  • The Complete Capitol Recordings of Art Tatum

    Art Tatum is considered one of the most skilled piano players to have ever lived. He was pure chops, and no track demonstrates his command of the instrument better than his performance of the standard "Blue Skies". It's hard to convince yourself that only one person is playing the piano.

  • Virtuoso #3

    There haven't been many guitarists (if any) in the preceding tracks. Here is one of my favorite jazz guitarists, Joe Pass, and I recommend "Passanova" from the album Virtuoso #3.

  • Waltz for Debby

    From the personnel of Kind of Blue, we have another one of my favorite pianists: Bill Evans. Here is his title track composition from the album Waltz for Debby with bassist Scott LaFaro and drummer Paul Motian.

  • Getz/Gilberto

    Here is some Latin Jazz you ought to recognize. This album was a collaboration between João Gilberto (singer and guitarist) and Stan Getz (saxophinist). Gilberto's then wife Astrud Gilberto had never sung professionally before this album, and the first track ("The Girl from Ipanema") became a worldwide success, launching her to superstar status.

    Notice I said "then wife"... Shortly after the album, Stan and Astrud had an affair, which brought an end to the both the musical partnership of Getz/Gilberto and to the partnership between João and Astrud (can't seem to find all the details of the story, but I'm pretty sure thats how it went down).

From here, I recommend going on Spotify and starting a jazz radio (you can start radios based off a song, so pick your favorite from above). If the radio playlist brings up a song you like, go to the album and listen to the whole thing. If you like that, check out their other work and "Related Artists". Also, get Dad's take! He has a much larger mental library of jazz than I do.
